The baseline correction we should make first.

Before the strategic argument, one calibration that changes the framing. The audit's $5.5M base is Meta-attributed, not company-wide. The $9M Stripe figure is total revenue across all channels — Meta, LinkedIn, affiliate, direct, partnerships. The Stripe figure runs materially above the Meta-attributed line — exact reconciliation requires raw channel data we'd pull as Phase 2 Move 0.

Expressed in total-company terms, the audit's Year-2 figure is materially higher than $8.8M. The "the plan lands us where we are today" framing compares a slice (Meta-attributed) to the whole (company total). That's a meaningful but recoverable misread. That said, the audit's ambition is calibrated to Phase 1 optimization — not to the $20M question. This proposal answers the $20M question separately.

Two clarifications that scope this proposal correctly: (1) The interactive model below projects Meta-driven acquisition only. Non-Meta revenue (~$3M today across LinkedIn, affiliate, direct, partnerships) continues separately and stacks on top as upside. The "$20M" figure refers to the Meta-driven engine. (2) The model implies meaningful ad spend growth — at Target scenario, 30K front-end buyers at $185 CAC = $5.55M/yr ad budget, up from current ~$3.4M. The two questions $20M requires answering.

$20M by Year 3 requires two things simultaneously, and they are not interchangeable:

  1. Grow the customer base — remove the $1,715 commitment barrier that caps Meta acquisition. A $1,715 entry price only converts buyers who can write the check on a Meta impulse, and that pool is small. The well runs dry at current ROAS levels.
  2. Monetize the base over multiple years — through recurring revenue, ascension upsells, and a community that members will pay to stay inside. Acquisition alone cannot do $20M; the math doesn't support it at Augment's CAC.

Optimization (the audit) addresses neither structurally. It improves Phase 1 economics but cannot remove the price-point ceiling or build the renewal stack. Both questions need a different offer architecture.

Why lifetime caps the model.

The $1,715 lifetime offer works today — it's a profitable transaction with $520+ contribution per buyer after CAC. But it has a structural ceiling that becomes visible at scale: lifetime buyers don't necessarily consume what they bought. They paid for it. It's theirs forever. There's no deadline, no renewal decision, no expiring access — so most defer engagement to "later," which often means never.

The downstream consequence is what kills the $20M path: if a lifetime buyer never consumed the product, you cannot sell them other things. The next-product pitch lands on someone who hasn't experienced the first product enough to trust the second. Workshop tickets, premium community, advanced programs — all of it falls flat on a member who never made it past Module 3.

The flywheel cost of lifetime
Lifetime maximizes acquisition revenue per buyer but minimizes engagement. The hidden cost isn't delivery — they already paid. It's the lost flywheel: no consumption means no testimonials, no referrals, no case studies, no expansion revenue.

A continuity product where engagement is structurally required fixes the flywheel. The proposal keeps lifetime as Rung 2 (it's still the most profitable single transaction Augment can run) and adds continuity as Rung 3. The two coexist because they serve different buyer mentalities — lifetime for the buyer who wants ownership, continuity for the buyer who wants ongoing access to people, programming, and updates.

The Day-One AOV principle.

Theo's read of the subscription tests is that "subscription doesn't work for us." That's the wrong conclusion. What the tests actually proved is sharper, and it generalizes to a principle the team can apply to every front-end decision they make.

Test 1 (cold annual sub, $399, ~$800 CAC, 0.5x ROAS) was doomed before it ran — not because subscription was wrong, but because the entire economic logic of subscription lives in the renewal. They never got to measure renewal. Selling cold means eating the full $800 CAC against a single $399 first period. That guarantees ~0.5x regardless of product quality. They didn't test subscription. They tested whether you can pay $800 to collect $399 once. The answer is always no.

Test 2 is better than the read suggests. The winning horse (AI Prompting) generated 150 buyers at ~$181 CAC against ~$27K spend — $22K front-end booked = 0.82x on the front end alone. Add the 10% sub take at $399 (~$6K) and that horse is ~1.0x on day zero, before a single renewal and before anyone converts to the $1,715 core. That's not a failing funnel — that's break-even acquisition with two unmeasured upside levers still in front of it. The blended 0.6x is hiding the winner because the dog offers (Fundraising, AI Strategy) drag the average down.

The principle that resolves the strategic disagreement
Day-one AOV must clear CAC. Whatever the front end is — subscription or one-time offer — the cash collected on the first transaction (entry + immediate upsells) must be ≥ acquisition cost. Everything behind that is profit.

Test 1 failed this rule ($399 day-one cash vs $800 CAC). Test 2's winning horse nearly passes it (~1.0x). The blanket "never sell sub cold" is too absolute. The precise version: never let day-one AOV sit below CAC.

Annual continuity sold to MBA grads + engaged front-end buyers (CAC ≈ 0). Community access, monthly faculty AMA, quarterly content drops, member directory, tool deals stack. The economic logic is sequencing. Rung 1 acquires cheap, qualified buyers and pays for itself on day one. Rung 2 collects upfront cash from the warm subset that's ready to commit. Rung 3 retains and monetizes the rest over multiple years — this is where the renewal compound lives. Every year, the recurring base starts with revenue already on the books, which is the structural difference between a $9M business that resets every January and a $20M business that compounds.

Continuity upsells (the LTV layer inside Rung 3)

The $1,500/yr base captures access. Per-member revenue compounds via four upsells:

  • Workshop Pass — $599/yr: all 12 monthly workshops included. Target take rate 35-45% among members. Pure add-on revenue.
  • À la carte workshop tickets: $199 member rate, $499-$999 non-member rate. Standalone revenue stream + acquisition channel (checkout upsell to membership).
  • Multi-year prepay at renewal: $2,499 for 2 years (saves $501, captures cash upfront, eliminates Year 2 renewal risk). Target take rate 25-35% of renewing members. Cash-flow mechanic — pulls Y2-Y3 cash into Y1 at renewal, doesn't create new revenue. Not separately modeled in the calculator since it doesn't change 3-year totals.
  • Lifetime Founder upgrade — $1,999: engaged members (90+ days active) can upgrade to lifetime continuity-equivalent access. Includes future modules + Workshop Pass renewable at $299/yr forever. Capped at 200/year. Net economic effect: captures upfront cash now AND modestly lifts LTV vs continuing renewals at 55% retention — expected LF LTV ~$4,000 vs expected continuing-sub LTV ~$3,000. Trade-off: trades steady recurring cash flow for lumpy upfront cash plus a long-tail $299/yr workshop stream.

Retention sensitivity — same inputs, retention varies
The structural insight: recurring revenue scales as 1 ÷ (1 − retention). A 25%-retention business and a 60%-retention business are not variations of each other — they are different companies built on the same ad spend. Industry standard edtech retention sits at 40-45%. The Target preset assumes 55% — above industry standard, achievable with the community investment described below, but not automatic. The whole bet is whether community quality lifts retention above the industry line. Model is directional — structure, not forecast.

Two notes on what's not in the math: (1) Year 1 figures assume mature steady-state acquisition. Realistic Y1 launch ramp from current $9M baseline to 30K-buyer velocity lands closer to $12-14M; treat the calculator's Y1 as the second-year "mature Y1" not the launch Y1. (2) The model includes ~$684K/yr of à la carte workshop revenue (8% of non-core front-end buyers × 1.3 tickets/yr × $249) baked into the front-end revenue line.

The retention bet — and what we're betting blind on.

The single most load-bearing variable in the entire model is continuity retention. Move the slider above between 25% and 65% — with everything else at Target preset, Year 3 swings from ~$18M to ~$21M. That's the difference between landing slightly under target and clearing it with cushion.

Augment has never measured continuity retention because they've never sold continuity. Industry standard edtech retention sits at 40-45%. The Target preset assumes 55% — above industry standard, defensible only if community quality is genuinely real. What kills retention: static content, weak community, no live programming. What sustains it: continuous new modules, monthly workshops, member-led peer programming, and a community where the relationships are the product.

Retention is the most load-bearing variable — but it's not the only unproven one. The model also assumes Year-3 acquisition velocity of 25K-30K front-end buyers per year — a meaningful scale-up from Test 2's 150-buyer cohort, requiring $5.55M ad spend (vs current $3.4M) and an OTO stack that clears day-one AOV (not yet built). There are two bets running in series: can we acquire at break-even on the front end, and will buyers stay long enough on continuity for the renewal compound to matter. Both have to work. Neither has been tested at scale.

What's measured vs what's assumed
The model has five inputs we don't have data on: continuity retention (never sold), continuity take rate from core (never offered), continuity take rate from non-core (never offered), Workshop Pass demand (never offered), and Lifetime Founder upgrade demand (never offered). Each is a directional estimate based on edtech category norms — not an Augment-specific measurement. The cheapest way to convert these from estimates to data is the warm continuity test described in "The next move" below — before the build, before the scale, before the spend.

The proposal builds in 4-6 new modules per year + 12 monthly workshops + ongoing community curation + community management staffing + event production. That's a $800K-$1.2M/yr content + community investment at the level required to sustain 55% retention and 25%+ take rates. Lighter investment ($400-600K range) lands the model at industry-standard retention (40-45%) and industry-standard take (10-15%) — Year 3 closer to $15-17M. The content and community commitment is not optional — it is the bet.

The cost picture (revenue model isn't a P&L)
The calculator shows gross revenue. Total operating cost at Target scale: ~$5.55M ad spend (visible in calculator) + ~$1M content/community + ~$1.5M delivery, support, platform, ops = roughly $8M total operating cost. Year 3 net contribution: ~$12M. The self-liquidating front end means Meta spend offsets at the AOV line, not as a separate deduction — so the contribution math holds even at higher buyer volumes.

The take rate concern (and the answer).

The honest concern with this model: take rates. Industry standard upsell from a paying customer base to a continuity tier is 10-15%. The Target preset assumes 26% from core buyers and 5% from front-end non-core — nearly double industry standard. Achievable with community as the gravitational pull, but not automatic.

A note on the non-core 5%: this isn't 5% of every front-end buyer regardless of engagement. It applies to the engaged subset — buyers who completed the tripwire, opened the email nurture sequence, and engaged with community previews but didn't take the $1,715 upsell. Realistically that's ~30% of front-end. Among that engaged 30%, the effective conversion to continuity is ~16-17%, which is industry-standard for warm community-led offers. The blended 5%-of-total reflects this combined logic, not a flat 5% across everyone.

At industry-standard take rates (15% core, 3% non-core, retention held at Target's 55%), the calculator lands Year 3 around $17M — misses $20M target by $3M. At industry-standard everything (Conservative preset: 10% core conv, 15% core take, 3% non-core, 40% retention), it lands closer to $13M. The fix isn't pricing or volume — it's community quality.

Best-in-class community-led subscriptions convert at 25-35% from core base because the offer isn't "more content" — it's "the people in this room are the operator network you need, and you can't get this anywhere else." That's structurally different from "join our community Slack." It requires:

  • Curated peer matching — specific intros between operators who can help each other, not "join Slack and figure it out"
  • Member-led programming — members teaching members, not just faculty broadcasting
  • High signal-to-noise — vetted membership, intentional channels, moderated discussion (the opposite of Discord)
  • Status and recognition — members get visibility to founders, faculty, each other
  • Real-world events — regional meetups, members-only dinners, an annual summit
The strategic investment decision
The path to $20M doesn't require more ad spend or new products. It requires investing in community quality at the same level as content production. If that investment happens, take rate hits 25-30% and the model exceeds target. If it doesn't, $17-18M is the realistic ceiling. That's the single biggest call Theo has to make.

What we considered and dropped.

Several mechanics surfaced in the analysis and didn't make the proposal. Documenting them so the team's questions are answered before they're asked.

Subscription as cold-acquisition front door (initial proposal, dropped)

Earlier drafts proposed a $499 Basic / $999 Pro subscription as the primary cold-acquisition mechanism on Meta. Dropped — but for a sharper reason than "cold sub doesn't work."

The Day-One AOV rule technically permits cold subscription if annual price clears CAC. A $999 annual sub against a $200 CAC would pass the cash-math test. The reason we still dropped it: cold buyers haven't experienced the product, so first-year churn runs much higher than warm-acquired subs — typically 60-70% first-year churn cold vs 35-45% warm. The day-one cash math passes; the Year-2 economics don't. Day-One AOV is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one — first-year retention also has to support the renewal year. Warm acquisition (from tripwire buyers or MBA grads) doesn't have this churn premium because the buyer has already experienced product value before subscribing. The three-rung structure with self-liquidating tripwire as cold acquisition is structurally cleaner.

Two-tier sub at $499 Basic + $999 Pro (dropped)

The two-tier mechanic added member count but split ARPU. Math: 60% take at $499 + 10% take at $999 generated similar total revenue to 30% take at $1,500 single-tier. Operational complexity wasn't worth it. Single $1,500 tier with paid upsells (Workshop Pass, Lifetime Founder) generates the same revenue with simpler product architecture, cleaner conversion math, and one community to manage rather than two.

$99/mo cancel-anytime monthly subscription (dropped)

The audit's earlier framing of monthly billing as the entry tier. Dropped because monthly cancel-anytime in edtech churns 5-8%/mo — average tenure 4 months, average revenue per monthly buyer ~$400, doesn't carry $400 CAC. Replaced with annual commitment (paid annually or monthly-billed against 12-month contract, same total price, no cancel-anytime option). Eliminates the 4-month churner segment entirely.

Credit-redemption tripwire-to-core upsell (dropped)

Originally considered: tripwire buyer's $149 becomes a credit applied against $1,715 within 30 days. Dropped. Buyer needs to understand 5 things to redeem a credit — what they paid, that it's now a credit, that it applies to a bigger product, that it expires, where to apply it. Most don't complete those steps. Direct upsell at standard price is a one-step decision and works better.

Major B2B / Team Licenses motion at launch (deferred)

Earlier drafts proposed building team licenses ($9,995-$25K/yr) as a major Year 1 revenue line. Deferred to Year 3. Building a corporate sales motion requires a full-time hire, longer sales cycles, and pulls focus from consumer funnel optimization which is the Year 1 priority. Year 1-2 should run inbound-only on team licenses (small contact link below order menu). Build outbound team sales in Year 3 once consumer funnel is mature.

What we would NOT do.

  • Don't convert the $1,715 lifetime into subscription. That trades a proven paid-upfront profit center for a leaky $399 that churns the moment members graduate. Most dangerous move on the table.
  • Don't sell continuity cold as the acquisition offer. Day-One AOV principle. Continuity belongs on warm acquisition only.
  • Don't scale any front-end before day-one AOV ≥ CAC. The AI Prompting horse is close (~1.0x). Finish the OTO stack engineering before scaling spend.
  • Don't build the entire model on any single retention assumption. Pressure-test the calculator at 30%, 45%, and 55% to see what survives. Industry standard is 40-45%; the Target preset assumes 55%. The community investment is what determines which scenario plays out.
  • Don't drop the entry price without pricing in the worse-avatar effect. A $149 buyer is not a smaller version of a $1,715 buyer — lower intent, higher churn, more support cost. Model discounts for this.
  • Don't clutter the funnel with four or five offers. Three rungs. Clean ladder.
  • Don't ask new cheap buyers to prepay 2-3 years upfront. Multi-year prepay belongs at the renewal / value moment, not at first purchase.
  • Don't ship faculty 1:1 sessions, in-person summit, or Founders Community as Year 1 launch products. They don't exist today. Build them in Year 2 once the sub base exists and demand is validated. Don't promise what isn't built.

The next move.

The cheapest, highest-signal experiment is not another cold Meta test. It's:

  1. Pull alumni repeat-purchase data. Augment has ~10,000+ lifetime buyers. How many have come back to buy a second product (tripwire, additional course, anything)? At what rate over what timeframe? This is the closest proxy available for what continuity retention might look like.
  2. Audit the 15 Test-2 sub upsell takers. Are they still active? What's their 30/60/90 day login rate? Content consumption rate? This is direct subscription engagement data — small sample, but the only sample that exists.
  3. Run a warm continuity offer to existing MBA grads. Offer $1,500/yr Augment Membership to existing lifetime buyers at near-zero CAC. Measure 30/60/90 day engagement. If the warm base won't stay, cold never will. If it does, the engine works and we size it confidently.

These three data pulls are 4-8 weeks of work and they gate the entire model. Before spending a dollar acquiring more customers, measure what retention looks like on customers Augment already has. Everything else follows from that number.
